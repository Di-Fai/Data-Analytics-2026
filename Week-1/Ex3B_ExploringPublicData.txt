1. Start by reviewing the data.gov home page. What is this? 
   -Data.gov is the U.S. government’s open data website. It gives the public access to datasets published by government agencies, so people can explore, download, and use public data for research, analysis, and decision-making.


2. Go to Most Viewed Datasets. How many datasets are listed?
    -There are 10 dataset categories and 402,371 datasets are listed.

3. Find the Topic Category for Human Health. Review the range of data sources 
available for this topic. Why do you think these were tagged with the Human Health 
category?
     -I think these datasets were tagged with the Human Health category because they relate to things that affect people’s health and well-being. Some of the datasets focus on food access, food prices, nutrition, air quality, public health vulnerability, and hospital care. Even though they come from different organizations, they all connect to human health in some way.

4. Go to the Food Price Outlook. Review the catalog page for this dataset. How can you 
navigate to additional datasets from the USDA?

    -From the Food Price Outlook catalog page, I can navigate to more USDA datasets by clicking the organization name, Department of Agriculture. This takes me to the USDA organization page, where I can browse many other datasets published by USDA.

5. Go back to the data.gov home page. This time, click on Datasets by Organization.
   -I went back to the data.gov home page and opened Datasets by Organization.

6. Try searching for your city, county, and state. What organizations are returned?
   -When I searched for New Castle, New Castle County, and Delaware, the results returned organizations such as the Department of the Interior / U.S. Geological Survey, the U.S. Census Bureau, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). My results may vary depending on the exact search term used.

7. Search for USDA, and click into the result. Within the USDA datasets, search for food composition. Notice that the top three results are all datasets concerning nutrition information. Try searching the USDA page for nutrition. How does that affect your results?

  a) One of the tricky things about working with data is that sometimes the terminology used by a data source is not obvious. You may need to dig around and be creative to find what you’re looking for.
     -When I searched USDA for food composition, the top results were nutrition-related datasets. When I searched for nutrition, the results stayed focused on nutrition but became broader and brought up more nutrition and dietary databases, such as Food Data Central and Food and Nutrient Database for Dietary Studies (FNDDS). This shows that changing search terms can affect how many and what kind of results you get.

8. Go back to the results for food composition, and click on Food Data Central. Review the catalog page for this data source, then click on the button for Visit Page. Take some time to explore the Food Data Central website.
   a) Try searching for butter, or any of your favorite foods. What kind of results do you get? What happens when you click on a result?
      -When I searched for butter, I got multiple results such as Butter, stick, salted; Butter, stick, unsalted; Peanut butter, creamy; and Sesame butter, creamy. When I click on a result, it opens a detailed page with nutrition information, serving sizes, food identifiers, and more information about that food item.

   b) Go back to the home page. Find Foundation Foods, and click on Downloads. What kind of data can you find on this page? How big are these files? Where can you find more information on the data types available?
     -On the Foundation Foods Downloads page, I found downloadable data files in CSV and JSON formats. The file sizes range from small zipped files in the hundreds of kilobytes to larger unzipped files in the megabytes. For example, one recent Foundation Foods release shows a JSON zip file of about 467 KB and an unzipped size of about 6.5 MB, while the CSV zip file is about 3.4 MB and unzips to about 29 MB. More information about the data types is available on the Data Type Documentation page and the Foundation Foods Documentation page.
