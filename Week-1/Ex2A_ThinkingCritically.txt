7. What does all of this suggest about the reliability of the conclusions reached in the 
article? 

   -This suggests that the conclusions in the article are not completely reliable and should be taken with caution.

One major issue is that Google Trends only shows search interest, not what people actually eat or prefer after a breakup. Just because a food was searched more in a certain state does not automatically mean it is that state’s favorite breakup comfort food. People could be searching for those foods for many other reasons, like cravings, recipes, or normal eating habits.

Another problem is that the data can change depending on what terms are being compared. As seen with milkshake, the line changed when another search term was added. This shows that Google Trends works with relative comparisons, not fixed results, which can make the article’s conclusion less solid.

Also, there does not seem to be a strong connection between searches for breakup and searches for foods like milkshake or hamburger. If the article wants to prove these are breakup foods, there should be clearer evidence showing that people are actually searching for them because of heartbreak.

Overall, the article seems more like a fun and interesting interpretation of search data than a fully reliable study. It gives an idea of possible trends, but it does not strongly prove that milkshakes are really the top breakup food in America.
